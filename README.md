The apple tree (Malus domestica) is a deciduous fruit tree valued for its edible fruit, ornamental flowers, and adaptability to temperate climates.
Description and Characteristics
Apple trees are generally small to medium-sized, deciduous trees in the Rosaceae family, typically growing 2 to 10 meters (6.5 to 33 feet) tall, depending on whether they are standard, semi-dwarf, or dwarf varieties. The bark is usually brown or gray-brown, with young branches reddish or dark-brown and smooth. Leaves are simple, oval to oblong, with finely toothed edges, and are alternately arranged on short stems. In spring, apple trees produce showy five-petaled flowers, mostly white with pink tinges, which attract bees and other insects for pollination. 

Fruit
The apple fruit is a pome, typically round, 5–10 cm in diameter, and can be red, green, or yellow. Apples are consumed fresh, cooked, or processed into products like juice, cider, vinegar, and preserves. There are over 7,500 cultivars, bred for dessert, cooking, or cider purposes, each with unique taste, texture, and color. 

Cultivation and Growth
Apple trees thrive in temperate climates with cold winters and warm summers, requiring a period of dormancy for optimal fruiting. They prefer well-drained, slightly acidic soils (pH 6.0–7.0) and full sunlight for at least six hours per day. Trees grown from seed may take 2–10 years to bear fruit, while grafted trees on rootstocks can fruit earlier and have controlled size and growth speed. Standard trees can reach 25–35 feet, semi-dwarf 15–20 feet, and dwarf varieties 6–10 feet. 

Pollination
Most apple varieties are not self-pollinating and require cross-pollination from another apple tree within about 25 meters (80 feet) to produce fruit. Bees and other insects are essential for transferring pollen between flowers. 

Pruning and Care
Pruning is important to maintain tree shape, improve sunlight penetration, and encourage fruit production. Apple trees are susceptible to fungal, bacterial, and pest problems, which can be managed through organic or chemical methods. Adequate water, especially during dry periods, is crucial for healthy growth and fruit development. 

Global Cultivation
Apple trees are cultivated worldwide, with major producers including China, the United States, and Turkey. They can grow in USDA Hardiness Zones 3 to 10, adapting to a wide range of climates from cold regions like Canada to milder areas in Africa. In the U.S., commercial production is concentrated in states such as Washington, New York, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and California. 

Life Cycle
The apple tree life cycle includes bud development, blooming, fruit setting, and ripening, with fruits maturing on shoots that are at least two years old. Trees can remain productive for 35–50 years under proper care. 

Apple trees combine ornamental beauty, nutritional value, and agricultural importance, making them a popular choice for home gardens, orchards, and commercial production worldwide.
